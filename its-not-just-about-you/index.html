It’s Not Just About You

Both of my children are teenagers. It often feels like they believe the world revolves around them. Don’t get me wrong, they are generous and kind. They care for people and I think they’re good friends. But, they are still teenagers and so at times, they believe the world revolves around them. 

I remember when the full reality of my parents divorce hit me. They were divorced when I was nine but it wasn’t until later that it really sunk in. I was probably 12 or 13 and I remember thinking, “Why me?” 

That seems to be the question we wrestle with when bad things happen to us. 

“Why me?”

Paul opens his second to letter the Corinthians this way, 

Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of mercies and God of all comfort, who comforts us in all our affliction, so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any affliction, with the comfort with which we ourselves are comforted by God. (2 Corinthians 1:3-4, ESV)

There are three things that we need to wrap our minds and hearts around. First, there will be affliction. Pain, sadness, sorrow, affliction are part of living in a fallen and broken world. It is the reality within which we live. The false teachers of our time say that if you have enough faith and claim things by that faith then you will be healthy and wealthy. It just isn’t true. Affliction is part of our experience and it will continue to be. 

Second, we have a comforter. Our Father in heaven comforts us in all our affliction. He cares for us. Through the Holy Spirit, “the great comforter”, we experience a comfort that is not our own. I don’t know how to explain it but, somehow we know that God is with us in our affliction. Perhaps it is through prayer or Scripture reading or the company of his people, most likely all three. But, we experience his comfort. 

Finally, we receive comfort to be a comfort to others in their affliction. You see it’s not just all about us. God comforts us because he loves us. He also comforts us to equip us to be an extension of himself. We learn from our experience how to come alongside those who are afflicted too. Because we have experienced comfort from our Father we can turn and offer that comfort to another. 

The beauty of the Christian life is that at its center there is connection and community. The Triune God, the one God who exists in three persons, is at the center of our faith. So it’s never just about one person. There’s always been an aspect of otherness at the heart of our faith. 

Over the years I have learned the answer I asked God as I came to grips with my parents divorce, “Why me?” As a pastor I can identify with and comfort people with the comfort that I ultimately received from my heavenly Father. Because of all the different things that I experienced growing up, I have found that I can be a much more caring, loving, patient, and effective pastor. The ability to relate to and identify with people in the midst of their affliction is a gift. I used to think it was a gift I’d like to give back. Now, I realize that it has made me who I am and is a means of grace. I wouldn’t trade it for anything. 

What are the afflictions that have made you who you are? They are gifts that you can turn and use to comfort others. Embrace them.
